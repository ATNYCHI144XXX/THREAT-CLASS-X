## Major Magnetic Anomalies

### 1. South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA)
**Location**: Atlantic Ocean, centered near Brazil-South Africa
- **Center Coordinates**: ~30°S, 30°W (moving)
- **Field Strength**: 50% below Earth's average (~25,000 nT vs 50,000 nT)
- **Drift Rate**: ~20 km/year westward
- **Area**: ~10 million km²
- **Impact**: 
  - Satellite malfunctions increased
  - ISS radiation exposure highest when passing through
  - Computer errors in aviation
  - Potential precursor to magnetic reversal

**Mathematical Model**:
$$B_{SAA}(t) = B_0 e^{-\lambda t} + B_{min}$$

Where:
- $B_0$ = initial field strength difference
- $\lambda$ = decay rate
- $t$ = time in years
- Current weakening: ~5% per century

**Threat Assessment**: HIGH
- Expanding westward
- Weakening accelerating
- May split into multiple anomalies

### 2. Kursk Magnetic Anomaly (KMA)
**Location**: Kursk Oblast, Russia
- **Coordinates**: 51.7373°N, 36.1873°E
- **Deviation**: Up to +2000 nT above background
- **Cause**: Massive iron ore deposit (banded iron formation)
- **Depth**: 400-500 meters below surface
- **Extent**: ~180,000 km²

**Effects**:
- Compass deviation within 250 km radius
- Aircraft navigation interference
- Geological survey complications

**Threat Level**: LOW (natural, understood, localized)

---

### 3. Bangui Magnetic Anomaly
**Location**: Central African Republic
- **Coordinates**: ~6°N, 18°E
- **Deviation**: +1000 nT above normal
- **Area**: ~700,000 km²
- **Age**: Possibly 540 million years old
- **Theory**: Ancient asteroid impact (Precambrian)

**Unique Features**:
- One of Earth's largest magnetic anomalies
- Circular pattern suggesting impact origin
- Deep crustal magnetization

**Research Significance**: 
- Understanding ancient impact events
- Crustal magnetization persistence
- Correlation with geological boundaries

**Threat Level**: LOW (ancient, stable)

### 4. Canadian Arctic Magnetic Pole Migration
**Current Location** (2025): ~86°N, 165°W
- **Historical Position** (1900): 70°N, 96°W
- **Movement Rate**: 55-60 km/year (accelerating)
- **Projection (2030)**: Crossing into Russia

**Acceleration Timeline**:
- 1900-1980: ~10 km/year
- 1980-2000: ~15 km/year
- 2000-2020: ~40 km/year
- 2020-present: ~55 km/year

**Mathematical Model**:
$$\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \vec{v}_0 + \vec{a}t$$

Current acceleration: ~0.5 km/year²

**Threat Assessment**: MEDIUM
- Navigation system updates required
- Aviation route adjustments
- Wildlife migration pattern disruption (birds, sea turtles)
- May indicate magnetic field instability

## Magnetic Field Reversal Indicators

### Current Global Field Status
- **Average Strength**: 48,000 nT (declining from 50,000 nT in 1900)
- **Decline Rate**: ~5% per century (accelerating)
- **Last Reversal**: Brunhes-Matuyama, 780,000 years ago
- **Average Interval**: 200,000-300,000 years
- **Overdue Factor**: ~3-4× typical interval

### Reversal Warning Signs
1. ✓ **Field strength declining** - confirmed
2. ✓ **Multiple poles forming** - SAA shows complexity
3. ✓ **Rapid polar movement** - North pole acceleration confirmed
4. ? **Increased reversal rate** - geological record suggests clustering
5. ? **Solar activity correlation** - under investigation

### Mathematical Probability Model
$$P_{reversal}(t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}$$

Where:
- $\lambda = \frac{1}{250,000}$ = inverse of mean interval
- $t$ = time since last reversal (780,000 years)

**Current Probability**: ~96% that we are "overdue"

**However**: Reversals are Poissonian (memoryless), so past time doesn't predict future time precisely.

### Reversal Timeline Estimate
- **Precursor phase**: 1,000-2,000 years (possibly current)
- **Transition phase**: 1,000-7,000 years
- **New stability**: 500+ years to stabilize

## Sites with Unexplained Magnetic Properties

### 1. Patomskiy Crater, Russia
- **Coordinates**: 59.283°N, 116.583°E
- **Magnetic Deviation**: Variable, +150-300 nT reported
- **Feature**: Unusual cone-shaped crater in forest
- **Age**: ~500 years (tree ring dating)
- **Theories**: 
  - Meteor impact (no meteorite fragments)
  - Volcanic gas explosion (no volcanic activity in region)
  - Unknown origin

**Status**: Requires systematic magnetic survey

### 2. Michigan Triangle / Lake Michigan
- **Area**: Southern Lake Michigan
- **Reports**: Compass anomalies, navigation issues
- **Verified Deviations**: Small, <100 nT, inconsistent
- **Likely Causes**: 
  - Local ferrous deposits
  - Shipwreck debris (iron-hulled ships)
  - Anecdotal exaggeration

**Status**: Needs controlled magnetic survey

### 3. Bermuda Triangle
- **Area**: Atlantic, Florida-Bermuda-Puerto Rico
- **Magnetic Claims**: Compass problems, anomalies
- **Reality**: Magnetic field in region is normal
- **Debunked**: No unusual magnetic properties
- **Actual Issues**: Heavy traffic, severe weather, Gulf Stream

## Mathematical Tools for Analysis

### Field Strength Calculation
$$|\vec{B}| = \sqrt{B_x^2 + B_y^2 + B_z^2}$$

### Dipole Approximation
$$\vec{B}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r^3}[3(\vec{m} \cdot \hat{r})\hat{r} - \vec{m}]$$

### Anomaly Magnitude
$$\Delta B = B_{measured} - B_{IGRF}$$

Where IGRF is International Geomagnetic Reference Field model.

## Contributing Magnetic Data

### Protocol for Reporting Anomalies
1. **Location**: Precise GPS coordinates
2. **Measurement**: Total field intensity in nT
3. **Equipment**: Magnetometer specs and calibration date
4. **Background**: Expected value from WMM for comparison
5. **Date/Time**: UTC timestamp
6. **Conditions**: Solar activity (Kp index), local disturbances

### Data Quality Standards
- Calibrated instruments only
- Multiple measurements (n ≥ 3)
- Statistical reporting (mean ± std dev)
- Comparison with IGRF/WMM baseline
